4/10/2026 - Rockets, Spaceflight, and Artemis II

Image: The Orion spacecraft launching for the Artemis II mission to reach the Moon. Credit: NASA

Artemis II and the Renewed Attention on Spaceflight

The Artemis II mission has lift off! We have successfully launched a rocket to send people to do a flyby around the Moon for reconnaissance for future Artemis missions, where either Artemis III or Artemis IV will land on the moon, marking the first manned Moon landing in over 50 years.

The Artemis program is doing many things: of course, we're studying the Moon by sending people there again, but it's also occurring at a time when STEM literacy is appearing to be on the decline in the US. I can't tell you the amount of videos I've seen about people debating over the "Moon landing hoax," flat earth, and other similar conspiracy theories. Now these conspiracies have been around for a long time, but now with social media playing such a large part in everyone's lives, the spreading of these conspiracy theories is reaching dangerous levels. The Artemis program should, ideally, help heighten the public's interest in learning about STEM subjects.

I've seen a large variety of people's "takes" on Artemis II: most people are ecstatic about the recent push towards space travel and that we can finally think about landing on the Moon again. There are also those that are still interested, but are not familiar with spaceflight (or arguably flight in general). There are those that are skeptical of the "realness" of the rocket launch and mission plan. Then there are those that are totally off the deep end and believe everything, including the Apollo Moon landings, is fake.

Now I'm no aerospace engineer or rocket scientist and I don't claim to be an expert at all in these fields, but I do have some working knowledge of why the rocket launch happened like it did (why did the rocket started turning sideways when space is straight up? Why is it taking Artemis II longer to reach the Moon than it did for many Apollo missions? Why did Artemis and Apollo reach the Moon, but Apollo stayed in a stable orbit around the Moon and Artemis didn't?). I'm not quite interested in addressing the mission plans and goals of each launch, I want to talk more about the mechanics of spaceflight, as it seems to be a topic of much confusion.

Play Kerbal Space Program!

I have a confession to make: most of my experience with spaceflight and my understanding of orbital mechanics and spaceflight comes from Kerbal Space Program. I know, I know, it's just a video game that simulates spaceflight using Newton's laws, but let me tell you, Kerbal Space Program is perhaps the most accurate and readily-accessible source most people have for learning about spaceflight.

Kerbal Space Program, a spaceflight simulation game developed by Squad, is an incredibly fun and mentally-stimulating game about building rocket ships and using them to fly to planets and Moons in the Kerbol system. It can be a sandox game where you have infinite money to make whatever rocket you want; it can be "science-based" where your goal is to rack up on scientific discoveries by visiting all the different biomes on each celestial body; it can also be more realistic in the sense that you have funding constraints and need to take on jobs and projects to fund more and more expensive missions. You are really only limited by your imagination (or your computer specs, as is the case with Stratzenblitz75)

I want to use Kerbal Space Program to explain some confusion I've seen online about spaceflight, as the simulations are accurate enough for us to do this.

Confusion Point #1: Why Did the Rocket Start Curving After Launching?

Image: The rocket launches going up straight, then turns as it gets higher. Credit: Reuters

One point of confusion I've seen online is people essentially asking "if space is straight up, why did the rocket start turning sideways?" This comes from a point of misunderstanding how orbits work. In simple terms: a rocket that is orbiting the Earth is essentially always falling, however it is moving horizontally so quickly that the ground keeps moving away from them (since the Earth is a sphere, no flat earthers allowed here). It is somehow "always missing the ground."

Isaac Newton did a thought experiment for this: consider a cannon on a spherical Earth. If it shoots a cannonball at some speed, the cannonball lands a distance away. If it shoots another cannonball at an even greater speed, the cannonball would land at an even farther distance away. It must be, then, that if the cannon shoots the cannonball with just the right amount of speed, the ball would travel so far that it loops around the Earth and hits the back side of the cannon.
