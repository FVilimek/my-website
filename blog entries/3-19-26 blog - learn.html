3/19/2026 - Process Controls is Vital to Engineering

Controls is at the heart of Engineering

I remember a conversation with my dad a few weeks ago and I think it summarizes my fascination with controls pretty well. My dad wanted to know about process controls since I chose to study it in grad school, and I was mentioning how important controls really is. He works at Tesla, and he was mentioning to me how some engineers were working on the heat transfer and thermodynamics of some car parts and wanted to know where controls played a part in that, and I said it plays an immense part in the application of their work---in some sense, it plays the most important part in bridging the gap between theory and application.

Consider a solid oxide fuel cell that is required to meet some power output. If you have an inlet stream of oxygen (which allows the fuel cell to generate power) that has a disturbance, you need to be able to accommodate that disturbance. Theory about the fuel cell by itself is not able to accommodate the disturbance.

Now consider a battery pack that must maintain a specific temperature range or else the batteries will degrade. You need to know how much cooling fluid to send over the battery pack to transfer heat away from it such that it maintains that temperature range. Heat transfer and thermo-fluid theory is incredibly important, but it still lacks in the implementation.

Next, consider a reactor containing an exothermic reaction that is producing a valuable compound. You need to have the outlet concentration of that compound to be high enough to efficiently separate it from the mixture, meaning you need to control the temperature within the reactor with a heat exchange jacket by controlling the flow rate of cooling fluid. Theory about chemical kinetics is important, but it says nothing about how a controller should be designed.

Lastly, consider a rocket ship launching to deliver a payload to the ISS. A rocket ship acts similarly to an inverted pendulum, and requires careful manipulation of the gimbaled thrust to both prevent the rocket from losing control and to achieve orbit using a minimal amount of fuel. Aerodynamics plays a huge role in determining the optimal route, but how exactly is the rocket going to follow that route?

The thing that solves all of these problems is controls. Controls is, I believe, at the heart of engineering, because at least in my opinion, the thing that separates science from engineering is in the application. Science finds the "why," engineering finds the "how." Chemists discover a new compound with very interesting properties; chemical engineers find out how to make it at scales of metric tons. Physicists discover a new theory for describing compressible fluids, aerospace engineers use it to design high-speed aircraft and rockets. In that same vein, a chemical engineer needs to scale up a reactor network, control engineers figure out how best to control the valves. An aerospace engineer needs to optimize aileron angles in turbulent conditions, control engineers figure out an algorithm that controls the aircraft with minimal instability. The one thing that connects theory to application is controls.

This might be a narrow-sighted point of view, and I apologize to anyone that this opinion offends. I just have such a firm belief that without controls, nearly all manufactured systems will be unstable and potentially dangerous. Controls is what makes a process design actually implemented.

Engineering is cool!

Enough about my rant about controls. Every STEM discipline plays extremely crucial roles in their own right. I do realize that my perspective is heavily geared towards an engineering mindset that seeks to create, as opposed to a more scientific(?) mindset that seeks to discover "why?" Now, as soon as my tirade about controls ends, my next tirade about engineering as a whole begins.

I suppose it comes as no surprise that without engineering, most things we know and love would not exist. Think about most, if not all, materials and substances you come into contact with every day. Think about car, motorcycle, boat, and aircraft engines. Think about satellites orbiting the Earth. Think about the waterways, dams, and public infrastructure. Think about all the electronics you use day-in and day-out. Think about the medical devices that keep people alive. The list can go on, and on, and on.

Pretty much as long as humans have been applying knowledge of science, they've also been inventing things using engineering principles to make their lives easier. Over milennia, engineering has blossomed into a field that ties (mostly) rigorous mathematics and cutting edge science to develop technology that seems almost like magic. Engineering essentially says "I will use all the math and science I have access to to make nature do what I want it to." Alright, I agree that sounds a bit power-trippy. It's also downplaying the amount of cross-disciplinary work that goes on in real life (tons of scientists and mathematicians can be and have become top-notch engineers, it's not like being an engineer requires some special gift). I just felt that I had to get it out of my chest that engineering is more than just a major you study in college or a job that your dad works with no other details. It's perhaps the field that has had the most impact on human lives in history, and though it can be ugly at times, at its heart it is beautiful.
