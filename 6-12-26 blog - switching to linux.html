6/12/26 - I Made the Switch to Linux

Image: "Tux," the penguin mascot of Linux. Credit: Larry Erwing (lewing@isc.tamu.edu).

The year of Linux may be approaching...

We truly do live in an interesting time. AI datacenters are being built on farmland, taking over small communities, and even some proposed to be launched into space. The increased surveillance and decreased privacy now are posing real concerns for more and more people---maybe it was always somewhat like this, but with the current agenda behind advancing an AI big brother, we should all start thinking about our privacy just a little bit more.

It is quite clear that companies are starting to care less and less about their customers and more about lining their pockets. I mean, not to get all "eat the rich" right now, but the big companies (Microsoft, Apple, Oracle, etc.) have billionaires (or at least multi-multi-millionaires) as their CEOs making decisions, and we know there's no truly ethical way to becoming that rich, so how can we trust that their business decisions will be entirely ethical? That statement might contain a logical fallacy but there's a lot of merit in it.

Anyways, Microsoft, Google/Alphabet, and Apple, three of the most well-known and overarching companies in the tech space, all seem to be going down the AI path. It's a slippery slope, and since you simply owning/using their products gives them permission to collect your data and secretly monitor your computer use at all times, I have a feeling like total government surveillance isn't too far away. That's not even being overdramatic, senile, or Orwellian. At the rate that tech companies are building AI datacenters and legislation is removing guardrails to make AI use more widespread, this seems like something that will happen.

I made the switch

With privacy in mind, I started by switching my browser from Google Chrome to DuckDuckGo. Chrome is super convenient and has all my extensions, bookmarks, and several passwords saved, so navigating the internet was quite simple, so the switch to DuckDuckGo seemed daunting. However, it honestly wasn't so bad. You can export all your bookmarks from Chrome and import them into DuckDuckGo, sign into your accounts, and it's kind of like nothing ever happened. I also opted to turn off all AI tools, so all I get when I search something up is the top results. Does this require more reading and thinking? Yes, but I would much rather use my brain than have a computer think for me.

After the browser switch, I got to thinking, "how hard would it be to switch to Linux?" I had the idea to switch to Linux after watching some LinusTechTips and SomeOrdinaryGamers vids over the years, and I had the opportunity to use a little bit of Linux at Intel (partitioning hard drives using GParted) and in school (supercomputers run on Linux, so we needed to know a few Linux commands for file manipulation and such for our stat thermo class) so I wasn't totally unaware of the ideology behind Linux.

The daunting thing that kept me from making the jump years ago was the steep learning curve---you have to know not only how to code, but also a little bit about operating systems and data structures and what have you. There was always that scary story you'd hear, "I accidentally deleted this major portion of my operating system and now my computer is bricked," so I stayed with Windows/MacOS for the time being.

But, the pressure from my privacy being violated paired with the fact that switching browsers wasn't so bad, I made the plunge. I had a USB with Linux Mint (shoutout to Chris Martinez) and thought, "what does Windows provide me that I truly can't access with any other OS?" and the answer was... nothing. So I followed some instructions on how to install Linux, formatted my hard drive to delete everything on it (after backing up the data of course), and now my PC is running Linux. No dual booting or anything, just Linux.

The learning curve is quite steep

As of writing this, I've had Linux for three days, and I've already noticed a bunch of things. First off, a lot of applications don't support Linux, so you have to find alternatives. Luckily, Linux is actually rather mature, so there's a lot of good alternatives out there for pretty much any application you can think of. Second, the large emphasis on customizability is a plus. There's tons of options for editing the themes of things, colors, icons, shapes of windows and panels, applets and desklets, you name it. You can make your desktop look and feel pretty much how you want it.

Then there's the file management and command line. This is where Windows/MacOS users, or those who aren't too tech-savvy, would have a lot of issues. You need to at least somewhat understand what all the different directories mean, how to install applications and run them through the command line, and other operating system stuff that you usually don't think about when using Windows/MacOS. If you don't know how to do something, be prepared to search countless StackOverflow or similar blogs of people solving similar problems (but probably not exactly the same problem). There's a lot of tinkering and troubleshooting involved, and if that doesn't suit your fancy, you probably shouldn't switch to Linux.

Three days in, I'm slightly getting a handle on things

Just this morning I feel like I've set up my Linux to have pretty much everything that I need. I have the apps that I use the most, my data from my old PC has been transferred over, I have a few games downloaded, and I'm using it pretty much exactly how I used my Windows machine, so there's been a negligible amount of change in my daily workflow. Like seriously, there is almost no change in how I do things now than when I used Windows, I'm actually baffled. Paired with the knowledge that my privacy is safer and I have more control over my computer, I don't think I'll be switching back to Windows anytime soon.

Will it take a while for me to get fully comfortable with the command line and all that Linux-y stuff? Yes, definitely. But I feel that after a few months of using Linux, I'll start getting a hang of things.
